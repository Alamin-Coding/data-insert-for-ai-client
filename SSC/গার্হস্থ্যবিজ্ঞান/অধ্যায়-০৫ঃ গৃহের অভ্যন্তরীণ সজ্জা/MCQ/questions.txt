Question:
Which principle is fundamental to quantum physics?

Options:
a. Theory of Relativity
b. Wave-Particle Duality
c. Natural Selection
d. Plate Tectonics


Correct Answer: b

Solution:
Solution:
Step 1: Identify the concept asked — quantum physics is principally concerned with the behavior of matter and energy at atomic and subatomic scales.  
Step 2: Key principle — wave–particle duality states that particles such as electrons and photons exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties; this is supported mathematically by de Broglie’s relation [math]\lambda = \dfrac{h}{p}[/math], linking wavelength [math]\lambda[/math] to momentum [math]p[/math].  
Step 3: Use evidence and elimination — experiments like the double-slit experiment and the photoelectric effect directly demonstrate wave–particle duality, so option (b) is correct. The other options are incorrect because the Theory of Relativity deals with high speeds and gravity (not the core quantum principle), Natural Selection is a biological theory, and Plate Tectonics is a geological theory.

Step 2 — Measurement: Upon measurement the superposed state probabilistically “collapses” to one of the basis states with probabilities [math]|α|^2[/math] and [math]|β|^2[/math], which shows the system was not in a single definite state before measurement.

Conclusion: This matches option a (a particle's ability to exist in multiple states at once). Options b, c, d are incorrect because b describes a definite (not superposed) state, c refers to particle decay (a different process), and d refers to speed, which is unrelated to the superposition principle.

What is the role of the observer in quantum mechanics?

Options:


Correct Answer: The observer affects the state of the system being observed.

Solution:
Solution:
Step 1: In quantum mechanics a measurement is represented by an observable (operator) [math]\hat{A}[/math] whose eigenstates satisfy [math]\hat{A}|a_i\rangle = a_i|a_i\rangle[/math].  
Step 2: If the system is initially in a superposition [math]|\psi\rangle=\sum_i c_i|a_i\rangle[/math], a measurement of [math]\hat{A}[/math] yields one eigenvalue [math]a_k[/math] with probability [math]|c_k|^2[/math] and the state collapses to the corresponding eigenstate [math]|a_k\rangle[/math].  
Step 3: Thus the act of observation (measurement) changes the state of the system — it selects (and forces) an eigenstate from a prior superposition.  

Brief note on other options: saying the observer only passively records or has no role contradicts the standard quantum result of wavefunction collapse and experimentally observed measurement disturbance, so those options are incorrect.
