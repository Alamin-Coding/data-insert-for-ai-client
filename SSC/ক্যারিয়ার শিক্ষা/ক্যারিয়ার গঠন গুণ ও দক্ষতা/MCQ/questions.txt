Quantum physics has no impact on everyday technology.

Correct Answer: b

Solution:
Step 1: Interpret the claim — it says quantum physics has no impact on everyday technology.  
Step 2: Test the claim with examples: modern transistors and semiconductors rely on quantum band theory and tunneling, lasers and LEDs depend on stimulated emission and electron–hole recombination (quantum processes), atomic clocks and MRI use quantum-level energy levels and spin.  
Step 3: Conclusion — because many common technologies are built on quantum principles, the claim is false. Correct answer: b (False).

Question:
What is a qubit?

Options:


Correct Answer: A qubit is the basic unit of quantum information, analogous to a classical bit.

Solution:
সমাধান:
ধাপ 1: সংজ্ঞা — কিউবিট হলো কোয়ান্টাম তথ্যের মৌলিক একক, যা ক্লাসিক্যাল বিটের অনুরূপ কিন্তু কোয়ান্টাম বৈশিষ্ট্য সম্বলিত।  

ধাপ 2: গাণিতিক প্রতিনিধিত্ব — একটি কিউবিটকে বলতে হলে সাধারণভাবে লেখা হয় [math]|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩[/math], যেখানে সংরক্ষণশীলতা বজায় রাখতে থাকে [math]|α|^2 + |β|^2 = 1[/math]। মাপলেযোগ্য ফলাফল 0 বা 1; তাদের সম্ভাবনা যথাক্রমে [math]|α|^2[/math] ও [math]|β|^2[/math]।  

ধাপ 3: তুলনা ও ব্যাখ্যা — ক্লাসিক্যাল বিট কেবল 0 বা 1 অবস্থায় থাকে, কিন্তু কিউবিট একসাথে সুপারপজিশনে থাকতে পারে এবং এনট্যাংলমেন্টের মতো কোয়ান্টাম প্রভাব দেখায়। তাই সঠিক উত্তর: "কিউবিট হলো কোয়ান্টাম তথ্যের মৌলিক একক, ক্লাসিক্যাল বিটের অনুরূপ"। অন্যান্য সম্ভাব্য ভুল বিবরণ (যেমন কেবল দ্রুততর বিট বা শুধুমাত্র একটি ভৌত কণিকা) যথার্থ নয় কারণ কিউবিটটি তথ্যগত ও কোয়ান্টাম-গত বৈশিষ্ট্য দ্বারা সংজ্ঞায়িত।

Question:
What is superposition in quantum physics?

Options:
a. The ability of a particle to be in multiple states at once
b. The principle of conservation of momentum
c. The state of being at rest
d. The concept of energy levels in atoms


Correct Answer: a

Solution:
Solution:
Superposition means a quantum system can be in a linear combination of basis states, e.g. [math]|\Psi\rangle=\sum_i c_i|\phi_i\rangle[/math], with measurement probabilities [math]|c_i|^2[/math]. This matches option (a) — "the ability of a particle to be in multiple states at once." Options (b), (c), and (d) refer to different classical/quantum concepts: (b) conservation of momentum is a symmetry law, (c) being at rest is a single definite state, and (d) energy levels describe allowed eigenstates rather than the general superposed state. Hence the correct answer is (a).

Question:
The Heisenberg __________ principle states that certain pairs of physical properties cannot be simultaneously known.

Options:
a. Equivalence
b. Uncertainty
c. Conservation
d. Relativity


Correct Answer: b

Solution:
Solution:
Step 1: Recognize the statement — it refers to the principle about limits on simultaneous knowledge of pairs of physical properties (like position and momentum).  
Step 2: This is the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle, which quantitatively is given by [math]\Delta x\,\Delta p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2}[/math], meaning the more precisely one knows position [math]x[/math], the less precisely one can know momentum [math]p[/math].  
Step 3: Therefore the correct answer is (b) Uncertainty. Options (a) Equivalence, (c) Conservation and (d) Relativity refer to different principles (equivalence principle in gravity, conservation laws, and Einstein's relativity) and do not express this limit on simultaneous measurability.

Question:
What role does observation play in quantum mechanics?

Options:


Correct Answer: Observation affects the state of a quantum system.

Solution:
Solution:
Step 1: In quantum mechanics a system is generally in a superposition, e.g. [math]|\psi\rangle=\sum_n c_n|a_n\rangle[/math], where [math]|a_n\rangle[/math] are eigenstates of the observable being measured.  
Step 2: A measurement (observation) causes the state to "collapse" to one eigenstate [math]|a_k\rangle[/math] with probability [math]P(a_k)=|\langle a_k|\psi\rangle|^2=|c_k|^2[/math].  
Step 3: Therefore observation actively changes the system’s state (selecting an eigenstate) and alters future evolution and measurement outcomes; it is not merely a passive reveal of a preexisting value.  
Why other options are incorrect: Any option claiming observation does nothing or only reveals a definite preexisting property contradicts the collapse rule and many experiments (e.g., interference disappearing when which-path is measured).

Question:
Which experiment demonstrated the wave-particle duality of light?

Options:
a. Double-slit experiment
b. Photoelectric effect
c. Millikan oil drop experiment
d. Rutherford gold foil experiment


Correct Answer: a

Solution:
Solution:
Step 1: Identify the experiment that produces an interference pattern when light passes through two narrow slits — the double-slit experiment. The interference maxima and minima satisfy [math]d\sin\theta = m\lambda[/math], which is a clear wave signature.

Step 2: Note the particle aspect: when the experiment is run with extremely low intensity (one photon at a time), individual detection events build up the same interference pattern, showing that light arrives as particles (photons) yet interferes like a wave.

Conclusion: These combined observations demonstrate wave–particle duality, so the correct choice is (a) Double-slit experiment.

Brief why others are incorrect:
- (b) Photoelectric effect demonstrates the particle (photon) nature of light and quantization of energy, not interference.
- (c) Millikan oil drop experiment measured the electron charge, unrelated to light’s duality.

b. Conservation of Energy

Correct Answer: a

Solution:
Solution:
1. Identify the fundamental concept: quantum mechanics is built on the principle that certain pairs of observables cannot be simultaneously known to arbitrary precision, i.e. the Uncertainty Principle (option a).
2. Key mathematical expression: Heisenberg’s relation [math]\Delta x\,\Delta p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2}[/math] (equivalently arising from the nonzero commutator [math][\hat{x},\hat{p}]=i\hbar[/math]) shows this limitation is intrinsic, not due to measurement error.
3. Reasoning: this principle follows directly from the operator formalism of quantum mechanics and underlies phenomena like wave–particle duality and quantization, so it is fundamental.
4. Why the others are incorrect (brief): Conservation of energy is a general symmetry law (Noether’s theorem) valid in many theories; Newton’s laws are classical and fail at atomic scales; the Theory of Relativity is a separate fundamental framework (relativistic physics) but not the defining principle of quantum mechanics. Therefore the correct answer is a.
