Question:
Reading books improves vocabulary. The word 'Reading' is a

Options:
a. gerund
b. phrase
c. participle
d. clause


Correct Answer: ক

Solution:
A gerund is formed by adding -ing to a verb and using it as a noun. Here 'Reading' functions as the subject of the sentence, so it is a gerund.

Explanation:
'Reading' comes from the verb 'read' + -ing and acts as a noun (the subject). It is not a phrase, participle (which would modify a noun), or a clause (which contains a subject and a verb).

Question:
We run because running keeps us fit. The underlined words are

Options:
a. verb -- adjective
b. verb -- gerund
c. verb -- present participle
d. verb -- noun


Correct Answer: খ

Solution:
The first underlined word 'run' is the base form of the verb (action). The second underlined word 'running' is the -ing form used as the subject (a noun) of the clause 'running keeps us fit', so it is a gerund.

Explanation:
A gerund is the -ing form of a verb when it functions as a noun. In the sentence, 'run' is a verb showing action, while 'running' acts as the subject (noun) of the verb 'keeps', so the pair is correctly classified as 'verb -- gerund'. Option c (present participle) would be correct only if 'running' were used in a continuous tense or as an adjective (e.g., 'running water'), which it is not here. Option d labels 'running' as a noun without specifying its gerund nature; the precise grammatical term is 'gerund'.

Question:
What do we call a verb that appears in an active form but conveys a passive meaning?

Options:
ক/a/ا. Causative
খ/b/ب. Ergative
গ/c/ج. Medio-passive
ঘ/d/ث. Quasi-passive


Correct Answer: ঘ/d/ث

Solution:
Quasi-passive. A quasi-passive verb has an active morphology but a passive semantic role: the subject is affected by the action rather than performing it.

Explanation:
A quasi-passive (also called pseudo-passive) is morphologically active but semantically passive. For example, in English sentences like "This book reads easily," the verb "reads" is in an active form, yet the meaning is that the book is easily read (passive sense). This differs from a causative (which expresses causing someone to do something), an ergative (relating to ergative alignment of arguments), and a medio-passive (which often indicates the subject acting upon itself or experiencing the action).

Which element is essential for forming a complete sentence in English?

Options:
a. auxiliary verb
b. principal verb
c. object
d. conjunction


Correct Answer: খ

Solution:

Question:
The soup smells delicious. Here 'smells' is-

Options:
a. a transitive verb
b. an intransitive verb
c. an auxiliary verb
d. a linking verb


Correct Answer: ঘ

Solution:
In this sentence 'smells' links the subject 'the soup' to the adjective 'delicious' that describes it. When verbs like smell, look, seem, feel, become, appear connect the subject to an adjective (or noun) they function as linking (copular) verbs.

Explanation:
A transitive verb requires a direct object (e.g., 'He smells the soup'). An auxiliary verb helps form tense/aspect (e.g., 'is eating'). An intransitive verb does not take a direct object but does not link to a subject complement in the way shown here. Since 'smells' here takes an adjective complement, it is a linking verb.
