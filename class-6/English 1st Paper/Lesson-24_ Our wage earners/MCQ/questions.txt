A lack of rainfall can cause rivers to __________.

Options:

b. Stop flowing
c. Collect more water
d. Spread wider


Correct Answer: খ

Solution:
Solution:
Step 1: Rivers are fed by rainfall (direct runoff, tributaries, and groundwater recharge).  
Step 2: When rainfall is lacking, the inflow and discharge of a river fall and can drop to zero, causing the river to stop flowing.  
Therefore the correct answer is b: Stop flowing.

Explanation of other options:
- a (Flow faster): Less water usually means lower discharge and often slower or ceased flow, not faster.  
- c (Collect more water): With less rainfall there is less water available to collect.  
- d (Spread wider): Rivers spread wider when there is excess water (flooding), the opposite of drought conditions.

Question #11 (ID: 67239)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Type: MCQ | Book: English 1st Paper | Years:  | Boards:  | Tags: 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Question:
How do prolonged dry seasons affect underground water levels?

Options:
a. They raise the levels.
b. They have no effect.
c. They lower the levels.
d. They stabilize the levels.


Correct Answer: গ

Solution:
Solution:
Step 1: Groundwater levels depend on the balance between recharge (mainly from rainfall/infiltration) and discharge (pumping, springs, evapotranspiration).  
Step 2: Prolonged dry seasons greatly reduce recharge while discharge often continues, so net outflow exceeds inflow and the water table falls.  
Step 3: Therefore underground water levels lower, so option c is correct. Options a, b, and d are incorrect because they require increased or unchanged recharge or perfect balancing of inflow and outflow, which does not occur during extended dry periods.

Question:
Explain how flooding might impact agriculture.

Options:


Correct Answer: Flooding can destroy crops and lead to food shortages.

Solution:
Solution:
Step 1: Floodwaters submerge and physically damage standing crops (rotting, broken plants) so immediate yields are lost.  
Step 2: Flooding causes soil erosion and leaches away nutrients, and waterlogged soil deprives roots of oxygen, reducing future productivity.  
Step 3: Floods also spread plant diseases and contaminants and delay planting/harvest, all of which reduce harvestable food and farmers’ income.  
Therefore, flooding can destroy crops and lead to food shortages. If an alternative option claimed only a short-term benefit (e.g., “adds water to fields”), note that any such benefit is usually outweighed by the direct crop loss and soil damage described above.
