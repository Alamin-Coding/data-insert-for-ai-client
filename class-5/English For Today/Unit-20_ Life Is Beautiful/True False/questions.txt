Question:
Maria is able to see the beautiful spring day because she is not visually impaired.

Options:
a. False
b. True


Correct Answer: a

Solution:
Solution:
Answer: a (False).

Approach:
We examine the logical claim “Maria is able to see the beautiful spring day because she is not visually impaired.” For a causal statement like this to be certainly true, the condition given (not being visually impaired) must be sufficient and necessarily lead to the ability to see at that moment.

Essential reasoning and steps:
1. Definitions: “Not visually impaired” means Maria has no lasting vision disorder. “Able to see the spring day” means she currently perceives the scene with her vision.
2. Sufficiency check: Absence of a visual impairment does not guarantee current ability to see. Many other factors can prevent vision temporarily or situationally (closed eyes, darkness, blindfold, sleep, temporary injury, occlusion, being indoors with no view, etc.).
3. Counterexamples that invalidate the causal claim:
   - Maria is not visually impaired but is asleep — she cannot see the day now.
   - Maria is not visually impaired but is in a dark room or blindfolded — she cannot see the scene.
   Each counterexample shows the antecedent (“not visually impaired”) can be true while the consequent (“able to see the spring day”) is false.
4. Therefore the stated reason is not a guaranteed cause of her seeing; the claim asserts a necessary causal link that does not hold in general.

Conclusion:
Because the given condition is not sufficient to ensure that Maria can see right now, the statement is false.

Explanation:
The statement is false because being able to see does not automatically mean a person is not visually impaired. Many people with visual impairments have partial vision or use glasses, contacts, surgery, or aids, so you cannot infer Maria’s impairment status solely from the fact she can see.

Type: True False | Book: English For Today | Years:  | Boards:  | Tags: 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Question:
Maria cannot feel warmth through the glass, which is why she doesn't know it's a nice day.

Options:
a. False
b. True


Correct Answer: a

Solution:
Solution:
Answer: False.

Answer approach:
1. Identify the claim: The statement asserts a causal link — because Maria cannot feel warmth through the glass, she does not know it’s a nice day.
2. Test the premises and causality using sensory and physical principles.

Essential steps and reasoning:
- Sensory evidence: People can judge weather by multiple cues besides tactile warmth. Visual cues (sunlight, clear blue sky, shadows, people wearing light clothing), auditory cues (birds, open windows), and indirect tactile cues (warm air near windows, sunlit surfaces) allow someone to know it’s a nice day without directly feeling warmth through glass.
- Physics of glass and heat: Glass transmits visible sunlight very well; it also transmits some near-infrared and absorbs/re-emits thermal radiation. Sunlight passing through glass can warm the glass surface and the air near it, so a person can often feel warmth by touching the glass or noticing warm air near it. Even if direct conduction through intact glass is limited, radiant heating and visual information still provide knowledge of sunny/nice conditions.
- Logical point about causation: The statement assumes inability to feel warmth through glass is the reason Maria doesn’t know it’s a nice day. Even if she couldn’t feel warmth through glass, that alone would not prevent other reliable indicators from informing her. Thus the causal claim is weak or false.

Example (brief): If Maria stands inside and sees strong sunlight and people outside in shorts, she can reasonably conclude it’s a nice day without needing to feel warmth through the glass.

Conclusion: The statement is false because (a) other sensory cues allow recognition of a nice day, and (b) glass often transmits or conveys enough thermal and visual information that the premise and the causal link do not hold.

Explanation:
False — not feeling warmth through glass doesn't by itself prevent knowing it's a nice day, because Maria can see sunlight, blue sky, or other cues that indicate good weather. Also, glass can transmit radiant heat, so lack of touch sensation isn't a definite reason.

Type: True False | Book: English For Today | Years:  | Boards:  | Tags: 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Question:
Maria's ability to learn is limited due to her visual impairment.

Options:
a. False
b. True


Correct Answer: a

Solution:
Solution:

Answer: False (option a).

Approach:
1. Identify the claim: the statement asserts that Maria’s capacity to learn is inherently limited because she has a visual impairment.
2. Evaluate the claim against relevant principles about disability and learning.

Reasoning steps:
1. Distinguish ability from access: Visual impairment affects sensory access to visual information, not the cognitive capacity to learn. Learning ability depends on cognitive functions, instruction, and access to educational materials.
2. Consider available accommodations and methods: With appropriate supports—Braille, large-print materials, audio books, screen readers, tactile diagrams, verbal descriptions, and specialised teaching techniques—people with visual impairments can learn the same concepts as sighted peers.
3. Evidence and examples: Many individuals who are blind or have low vision succeed academically and professionally by using assistive technology and inclusive teaching methods (e.g., using Braille for reading/writing, screen-reading software for digital content, audio lectures).
4. Principle: The social/relational model of disability views limitations as arising from barriers in the environment and lack of accommodations, not from the person’s intrinsic learning capacity.

Conclusion:
Because visual impairment does not inherently reduce Maria’s ability to learn—only her access to visual formats unless accommodations are provided—the statement is false. Therefore the correct choice is option a.

Explanation:
False — Visual impairment does not inherently limit a person's ability to learn; with appropriate accommodations (Braille, audio materials, assistive technology, and adapted teaching methods) learners with visual impairments can acquire knowledge and skills just as effectively as others.
